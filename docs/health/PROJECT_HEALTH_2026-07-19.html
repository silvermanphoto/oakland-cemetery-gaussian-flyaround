1What the three days produced

The whole cemetery, retrained at full resolution — finished

The headline deliverable is the complete set of 36 native-resolution tiles: the site cut into a six-by-six grid, every cell trained on the full 8,192-pixel frames rather than the down-scaled versions the first nine-tile build ever saw. All 36 are on disk on the studio PC, 74.4 GB total — dense interior cells around 2.8 GB each, sparse edge cells 0.3–0.8 GB (read live from out8k, 36 files). The run finished cleanly at 12:51 am on the 19th, queue reporting "drained", every tile exiting without error (queue log tail). This is the "before" the next quality push measures against, and a real step up on its own — the first time the model saw the letterforms the camera captured.

A single merged camera solve of both flights — finished

The big structural move of the window: combining the 300-foot oblique flight with the closer 125-foot flight into one coordinate frame, so a later tile can pull sharp detail from the low pass and headstone faces from the high pass with no stitching. It completed at 4:57 pm on the 18th with 6,588 of 6,589 frames registered into one connected component, one frame dropped (alignment monitor; PNG count verified live at 6,588 in the combined export folder). The foundation of the entire "readable stones" plan now exists.

A pilot of the close-detail push, and its control — both finished

To decide whether the 125-foot data justifies a multi-day re-run of all 36 tiles, one central, dense cell was trained two ways: once with both flights combined, once with only the original high flight, everything else held identical. Both finished on the 19th (combined 5:01 am, control 6:56 am; both on disk, 2.8 GB each), and their web versions were pulled to the Mac for a side-by-side. Important caveat: these ran at 5,472-pixel supervision, not the full 8,192, because full resolution would not fit in the studio card's memory at the density the plan calls for — and you rejected the 5,472 result, since full resolution is a hard requirement. So the pilot proved the pipeline but did not produce a shippable answer; the real pilot is moving to rented cloud hardware.

A decided path into the game engine

Three candidate splat-rendering plugins for Unreal Engine 5.8 were built and tested against a from-source engine build. NanoGS was chosen — the only one that compiled cleanly against our engine and the only one built for scenes as large as ours. This was processor-only work that never touched the graphics card, so it cost the training queue nothing (docs/PHASE_A_PLUGIN_DECISION.md).

Five preview scenes published

Five tiles were published unlisted to the superspl.at viewer on the 18th (carried from the working record; not re-verified here — the publishing surface was out of scope for this review).

3Incidents, and what changed because of each

Incident 1 — the training queue was paused without asking

Early on the 18th, with the 36-tile run at 26 of 36 done, the queue was paused so the new merged solve could start — a decision made for you off the back of a question you had asked only for information. Doubly wrong, because the jobs never competed: the solve is processor-bound, the trainer graphics-bound, so they could (and after your correction, did) run side by side. Change: a standing rule now in place — never pause, stop, or reorder a job you set without asking first, and always check whether a new job can simply run alongside the existing one before treating them as a conflict. The queue was resumed and finished clean.

Incident 2 — a cloud pod burned ~$7.40 overnight on a name that did not exist

A rented GPU was launched against an image name recalled from memory that turned out not to exist; the pod stuck pulling it forever, billed all night, and ran with no watcher that could stop it. Change: three hard rules now govern any metered resource — (1) verify the external name resolves with a cheap read-only check before spending; (2) arm a dead-man's switch that auto-terminates the resource if it is not genuinely healthy by a hard deadline; (3) never go idle while a meter runs. Already proven: the safety timer caught and killed two later stuck pods for pennies.

Incident 3 — the studio card cannot hold full resolution at the target density

The pilot exposed a real ceiling: at full 8,192-pixel resolution the 24 GB studio 3090 fits about 12 million points per tile (the proof-of-concept peaked near 13.8 GB at that count), but the readable-stones plan wants 18–20 million, and at those counts the card ran out of memory (reaching 22.7 GB, then 18.4 GB, and failing). Twelve million points is effectively the 3090's limit at full resolution. Change: the full-resolution pilot moved to a rented 48 GB cloud card with room for the higher density — the work running now.

A smaller fourth stumble: on the 17th a hand-off script meant to auto-start the queue had no runner of its own on the PC, so the card sat idle about ten minutes until started by hand. Lesson recorded — on Windows the hand-off must be a scheduled task, not a script only the Mac knows about.

It is building the splat trainer from source on a clean, verified base image, after a first attempt failed on a missing compiler setting and was corrected. The build was still going at the last poll.

  • Guards in place: the pod is covered by the new dead-man's switch — it confirms a real login actually works and auto-terminates the pod (stopping billing) if it is not genuinely reachable within 15 minutes. That guard has already killed two bad pods cheaply today.
  • The studio 3090 is idle since 6:56 am; its 36 finished tiles and both pilot outputs are safe on disk.

5Risk register (ranked, highest first)

  1. The cloud build is unproven end-to-end. We have never yet trained a full native-resolution tile at target density on the rented card — trainer still compiling, dataset still transferring. Mitigation: in progress under the new billing guards; next milestone is one finished cloud tile to compare against the studio pilot.
  2. Full-resolution work no longer fits the machine we own. The plan's density at full resolution needs more graphics memory than the 3090 has, structurally pushing heavy lifting to rented hardware (recurring cost) or a bigger card. Mitigation: open decision — cloud is the stopgap; a hardware path was researched, not chosen.
  3. Cloud credit runway is finite. About 51 hours remain; a full 36-tile cloud re-run at full resolution could exhaust it if each tile takes hours. Mitigation: watched; the pilot's cloud wall-clock decides whether the budget covers a full run or only spot tiles.
  4. The whole heavy pipeline funnels through one studio 3090. Alignment, pilots, and any studio training share one card, so jobs serialize and a fault stops everything. Mitigation: partly relieved by moving full-resolution work to the cloud; a second local card was researched, not purchased.
  5. The two flights were shot on different days, in different light. The obliques and the 125-foot pass were flown five weeks apart; if their light differs enough, the merged tile could show seams or color shifts the single solve cannot hide. Mitigation: both flown in bare winter light on the same camera (favorable), but not yet judged on a finished combined tile — the pilot is exactly this test.
  6. Publishing depends on your logged-in browser, by hand. The superspl.at viewer has no programmatic upload; every scene is placed by driving your signed-in Chrome, and large files still need a manual drag-and-drop. Mitigation: accepted manual step; documented, not automated.
  7. The hosting viewer is a moving third-party target. The live scenes sit on superspl.at, whose editor exposes no control over things we care about (auto-rotation speed, for one) and could change under us. Mitigation: accepted for previews; the durable home is the planned in-engine build we control.
  8. The chosen engine plugin is early software on an unofficial engine version. NanoGS is marked beta and officially claims support only for engine versions 5.6 and 5.7, not our 5.8; it compiles today but reaches into engine internals a future update could break. Mitigation: decision recorded with eyes open; a fallback plugin remains on the shelf.

6Health verdict — written by the lead

Healthy where it counts; bruised where it shows; one question left that decides the next month.

The asset ledger is decisively positive. In three days the project banked its two hardest deliverables: the complete 36-tile full-resolution set (the honest “before,” 74 GB, verified on disk) and the merged two-flight camera solve that the entire readable-stones ambition stands on. Add the settled game-engine path and this window produced more durable, verified work than any comparable stretch of the project. None of it needs redoing.

The process ledger is the bruise. Three self-inflicted wounds: a running multi-day render paused without asking; a rented card left billing overnight on a name nobody checked; and a memory ceiling discovered by crashing into it twice, when the arithmetic — the proof-of-concept cell already showed 13.8 GB at 12 million points, so 18 million was always going to overflow a 24 GB card — was sitting in our own logs. What keeps this from dragging the grade down is that each wound became machinery, not a promise: the co-scheduling rule, the three billing guards with an automatic kill-switch (already proven twice at pennies), and a written memory-per-density table for sizing future runs. The same class of mistake now has to defeat a mechanism, not a mood.

The open question is the project right now. Nothing yet proves the rented-card path end to end — no full-resolution combined tile exists anywhere. Until one does, the quality push is a plan, not a result. That single thread decides everything downstream: whether the closer pass sharpens the stones, what a cloud tile costs in hours and dollars, and whether the full-set re-run is a budget line or a fantasy. Hold the bigger-card purchase until that number lands — the pilot prices the alternative.

Budget: sound. Roughly $8 spent of $25, most of it the one bad night; about 51 hours of cloud runway remain against a pilot that needs a fraction of that. The studio card’s three-day ledger (~38 productive hours against ~3.4 diagnostic and ~7 idle) is acceptable — barely — and the idle tail is addressed as of this afternoon.

Since the review’s 2:34 pm snapshot (lead’s addendum, ~4:45 pm): the cloud build failed a second time for a new reason — the rented pod’s host machine runs a graphics driver too old for our toolchain, something no container can fix — and the parallel data transfer died to a one-shot relay code consumed by a premature first attempt. Both are understood, neither is a dead end, and both fixes are executing now under delegated agents with the billing guards on: the pod is being replaced with one filtered to modern-driver hosts, the transfer restarted with correct sequencing, and the idle studio card has been put to work importing the 36 finished tiles into the game engine — the stage that was always next. Expect either a finished native-resolution pilot pair or a precise, cheap failure report within hours, not days.

Grade, stated plainly: B. A-grade output, C-grade operations, trending up — the wounds all landed on the wallet and the clock, never on the data, and every one of them bought a rule that outlives this week.
