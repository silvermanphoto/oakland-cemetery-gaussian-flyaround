The material this was built on. Oakland is a 48-acre historic Atlanta cemetery Joel photographed from a drone in December 2022 — two flights on the same 44.7-megapixel camera: a 300-foot oblique orbit of 3,270 frames and a lower nadir pass of 3,319 frames (counted live on the studio PC). Joel had already turned that capture into photogrammetry meshes himself in 2023 — a stack of RealityCapture projects dated January–February 2023, one labelled "both maps combined but weird fishbowl going on" and its "redo no bowl effect" follow-up (project files on disk). This collaboration started from a rich archive and a known-hard problem — merging the two flights cleanly — that Joel had personally wrestled with three years earlier. The 2026 work is the Gaussian-splat answer to that same material.

1Ambition and scope stewardship

The project's reach grew enormously over five days, and the record shows the growth was deliberate and Joel-driven at every step, not drift. It opened bounded and well-specified: nine dense splat tiles plus one fused master, delivered to a web viewer, with "zero floaters, then maximum detail" written down as the ordering principle (docs/MASTER_PLAN.md). That goal was met. The first escalation — from the down-scaled first build to training at the photographs' full 8,192-pixel resolution — was Joel's explicit upgrade on July 17 ("Joel's upgraded requirement, 2026-07-17 — no downscale"; sandbox CLAUDE.md). The second — nine tiles into thirty-six for four times the density — followed the same written plan. The third — merging the closer 125-foot flight so inscriptions become readable, aiming at a walkable game-engine world — is dated to when Joel saw the full-resolution tiles (docs/QUALITY_PUSH_125FT_PILOT.md).

The stewardship strength: each leap was gated by a pilot rather than committed blind — the whole 36-tile re-run is held behind a single pilot cell so Joel can judge before days of compute are spent. The one place ambition outran discipline was procedural, not directional — reaching for the merged solve by quietly pausing a run Joel had already set, rather than asking (Subjects 5 and 8). The direction was right; the manner was the fault.

  • Strongest moment: the readable-stones plan opens by naming its own ceiling plainly — at 300 feet a one-inch letter lands on one or two pixels, so no training invents detail the pixels never held; source resolution first, density second. Ambition anchored to physics.
  • Weakest moment: treating an exploratory "can we push further" as license to re-order a running job — scope stewardship failing on process even when the idea was sound.

GRADE: A-

TEACHER'S COMMENT: Every escalation was the artist's call, anchored to physics, and gated by a pilot before days of compute were committed — model behavior. Docked for once taking the right destination by the wrong route.

2Reconstruction quality

The quality trajectory is real and measurable. The camera solve is the load-bearing asset and it is strong: the nine-tile era registered all 3,270 frames into one coordinate frame, and the July merged solve registered 6,588 of 6,589 frames of both flights into a single connected component (health review §1; export folder verified at 6,588 images). One shared solve is what lets independently trained tiles meet at their seams with no stitching — the single most important quality decision in the pipeline (README).

Density climbed across three eras, with the assets on disk to prove it: nine dense tiles at roughly 4–6.4 million points each (11.9 GB across nine files), a fused 52-million-point master (11.9 GB, dated July 16), ten web scenes totalling 1.26 GB (counted live in deliver\), then 36 tiles at 74.4 GB trained on the full 8,192-pixel frames the first build never saw.

The caveat that shapes the grade: density and solve are verified, but the legibility claim — that stones actually read now — is not yet proven on a finished full-resolution combined tile. The pilot built to test it ran at a reduced 5,472-pixel resolution (the studio card could not hold full resolution at the target density) and Joel rejected that fallback, so the readable-stones result is still pending on cloud hardware (health review §1, §3). Quality delivered is excellent; quality claimed for the next tier is still a hypothesis under test.

  • Strongest moment: a 100%-registered global solve carried from nine tiles to thirty-six to a two-flight merge without ever re-aligning — seams free by construction.
  • Weakest moment: the full-resolution-plus-density target does not fit the machine it was designed on, so the headline "readable stones" deliverable does not yet exist.

GRADE: A-

TEACHER'S COMMENT: One frame lost out of 6,589 across two flights flown five weeks apart — the solve work is genuinely excellent, and the seams-free-by-construction decision will pay dividends for the life of the project. The minus is simple: the promised next tier does not exist yet, and promises are not assets.

3Pipeline and tooling engineering

This is the collaboration's deepest strength. A genuinely hard multi-stage pipeline was built and its sharpest edges mapped rather than stumbled over twice. The tiling math: at 300 feet every camera sees the whole site, so the obvious "which cameras see this tile" test selects the entire flight — useless; the working method assigns each camera by where its view axis hits the ground plus a containment test, giving clean per-tile subsets (nine-tile lesson 3; README). Boundaries are cut by content density, not geometry.

The web-export tool's undocumented behavior was reverse-engineered into durable rules: its translate flips the Z sign, its box-crop reads X corners in a negated frame (crop to a region by passing the corners negated and swapped), stats print the pre-transform numbers, and the crop must be the final action — each cost real debugging and each is now one line (nine-tile lesson 4; sandbox CLAUDE.md). The trainer was tuned to the hardware and the failure modes: JPEG-on-local-drive over PNG (a 28-fold throughput difference), the mrnf strategy to dodge a driver deadlock, an ordered out-of-memory fallback ladder.

Many pieces became reusable assets: a resumable tile queue with skip-done markers and stall detection, a prominence detector that finds each tile's hero monument, a partition script reused verbatim for the 6×6 subdivision, a whole Windows-unattended discipline. The one enduring rough edge: the tile files are named on a scrambled grid — a tile's name does not match the cell its geometry occupies — which a sibling process had to detect and work around at export (ue-import-notes.md).

  • Strongest moment: the 28-fold feeding speedup — diagnosing that PNGs starved the GPU at 20% while CPUs decoded, and JPEG-on-NVMe pins it at 97%. One insight, a day of compute saved per era.
  • Weakest moment: the scrambled tile-name-to-cell mapping — a data-integrity foot-gun contained but never fixed at the source.

GRADE: A

TEACHER'S COMMENT: The strongest work in the class: hostile, undocumented tools were mapped into one-line rules instead of being stumbled over twice, and a 28-fold speedup was found by actually reading the log line everyone else scrolls past. Fix the scrambled tile names before they bite someone who wasn't there.

4Resource and budget stewardship

Graphics-card accounting for the whole arc: nine dense tiles cost roughly 3.75 hours (nine tiles at the README's ~25 minutes each — an estimate from the stated rate, not separately logged), the 36 full-resolution tiles cost ~34.6 hours (summed from the queue log), and the pilot-and-control added ~3.4 hours — about 42 GPU-hours of real work across five days, the large majority productive (health review §2). The merged solve added ~13 hours of processor time that, after correction, ran alongside the trainer rather than blocking it.

The waste is small in proportion but real and named. On the card: a ~10-minute idle gap on July 17 from a hand-off script with no runner, the ~3.4 diagnostic hours whose 5,472-pixel output Joel rejected, and a roughly eight-hour idle window on July 19 after the pilots. On dollars: a cloud account funded at $25.00, now $17.05 (live query), of which about $7.40 was pure waste — a pod launched overnight against a container-image name that did not exist, billing all night while stuck, unmonitored (health review §2–3; memory "guard billed resources"). That is the worst stewardship failure of the project — and it produced the strongest corrective.

To the credit side, the response was disciplined: a dead-man's-switch watcher now auto-terminates any pod not genuinely reachable by a hard deadline, and it has already killed two later bad pods for pennies. The remaining balance buys about 51 more hours — adequate for a pilot, tight for a full cloud re-run.

  • Strongest moment: turning the $7.40 loss into three enforced billing rules plus a proven auto-kill guard within the same day.
  • Weakest moment: the loss itself — real money spent overnight, unwatched, on a name never verified.

GRADE: C+

TEACHER'S COMMENT: Forty-two productive hours do not excuse a meter left running all night on an unchecked name, and an owned card idling while a rented one works is the same lesson in a smaller font. The rules written afterward are excellent; they would have graded higher written before.

5Operational reliability and recovery

Reliability was rocky in absolute terms but the recovery discipline was consistently good — nearly every failure produced a written, durable rule. In the nine-tile era: a Windows Update reboot mid-run installed a graphics driver (591.86) under which the default densification strategy deadlocks the instant the point count reaches its cap — reproducible, sometimes leaving an unkillable process holding the card until reboot; the cure (switch to mrnf, never hard-kill a running trainer) is now standing (nine-tile lesson 2). A hand-off script with no runner left the card idle ~10 minutes. In the full-resolution era: the queue was paused at 26 of 36 without asking (Subject 8), and the out-of-memory ceiling was found the hard way — about 12 million points is the 3090's limit at native resolution; 14–18 million overflows (health review §3). In the cloud era, three failures in one day: the ~$7.40 stuck pod; a replacement pod whose host graphics driver was too old (550 against a required 570), caught and swapped for a confirmed newer driver (cloud-pilot-notes.md); and a file-transfer that kept dying instantly on stale compressed leftovers, diagnosed and cleared.

Time-to-detection improved markedly. Early failures were caught by postmortem after the fact; by the cloud era the guards were preventive — the too-old driver caught before a build was wasted, stuck pods auto-killed by deadline, the transfer blocker found on the first failed send. The system learned to fail cheap and fast.

The grade weighs two things: the raw failure count is high and several were self-inflicted (an unverified image name, a paused job) — but the recovery quality is genuinely strong, most incidents now one-line rules a future session inherits, and the guards demonstrably work.

  • Strongest moment: the billing dead-man's switch killing two bad pods for pennies hours after the rule was written — a fix that immediately paid for itself.
  • Weakest moment: the driver-591.86 deadlock plus an unkillable card — a failure clearable only by rebooting the machine, the least graceful recovery on the list.

GRADE: B-

TEACHER'S COMMENT: Falls down often; gets up well. What keeps this grade off the C bench is the trend line — from postmortems that explained yesterday's loss to guards that kill today's failure for pennies before it becomes one.

6Presentation and publishing

The public face is strong and improving. Ten scenes went live on the web viewer from the first build — nine tiles plus a fused whole-site master, all unlisted (docs/LIVE_URLS.md). The viewer experience was tuned to real lessons: each tile is recentered so the orbit pivot lands on that tile's hero monument (a prominence detector finds obelisks, ignores tree canopy), and every tile is scaled down about twenty-fold because the viewer's pan and zoom assume small scenes and a 290-meter tile otherwise feels dead (nine-tile lessons 5–6). Orientation was diagnosed from monument anatomy — an obelisk tapers upward — after a pre-rotation once shipped scenes upside down.

The repository itself is a presentation artifact: at Joel's explicit direction a public GitHub repo (an exception to the private-by-default rule), its README in his own first-person voice, opening on the December 2022 flight and Margaret Mitchell's grave rather than on tooling (README.md).

One preference could not be honored: Joel's slow, contemplative auto-rotation lives only in viewers we control, because the web viewer exposes no rotation control (memory "splat rotation speed") — a platform limit, correctly identified rather than faked. Publishing itself stays a careful manual process — every scene placed by driving Joel's signed-in browser, the largest file by human drag-and-drop.

  • Strongest moment: the data-driven viewer UX — hero-monument orbit pivots and the 20× navigation scale — turning a raw point cloud into scenes that open on an artful, legible oblique.
  • Weakest moment: publishing remains hand-driven through a logged-in browser — a step that cannot run unattended and gates every release on Joel's presence.

GRADE: A-

TEACHER'S COMMENT: Scenes open on monuments instead of haze because someone did the math on where the beauty lives in the data — that is presentation earned, not decorated. The hand-driven publish step is the one door still requiring the artist's key, and the platform's missing rotation control was named as a limit rather than papered over.

7Documentation and institutional memory

The written record is unusually thorough and is the project's quiet triumph. The nine-tile build was governed by a 69 KB master plan with ten numbered stages, hard gates, and a section reconciling every reviewer objection (docs/MASTER_PLAN.md). The hard-won lessons of each era are captured as numbered, costed rules — ten for the nine-tile build alone, each "paid for in hours" — covering the feeding trap, the deadlock, the camera-assignment method, the transform-tool quirks, the viewer UX, and the Windows-unattended discipline. Live-scene ledgers, phase decision memos, and this review's own dossier deepen the paper trail.

The test that matters — could a cold session restart from the written record alone — is largely yes: the machine facts, paths, exact trainer commands, transform gotchas, and recovery rules are all written where a fresh session would look, and the memory files carry the behavioral rules alongside the procedure.

The gaps are minor: the nine-tile era's exact graphics-card hours were not logged (hence an estimate in Subject 4), and one live document has drifted — the readable-stones pilot plan still names the wrong pilot cell (tile_2_3 where the work used tile_2_4) and quotes point-count targets from before the memory ceiling was found (QUALITY_PUSH_125FT_PILOT.md). Stale docs are the tax on moving fast; here the tax is small.

  • Strongest moment: a fresh session genuinely can cold-start — the exact commands, paths, machine quirks, and behavioral rules are all in the written record.
  • Weakest moment: a still-live plan document names the wrong pilot cell and pre-ceiling targets — a small correctness drift in an otherwise excellent record.

GRADE: A

TEACHER'S COMMENT: The rare project a stranger could pick up cold and run — commands, quirks, costs, and conduct all in the binder. One stale page, corrected the same day the review found it. This is what institutional memory is supposed to look like.

8Collaboration and communication

The collaboration ran mostly well, with a handful of pointed corrections the record shows were learned rather than repeated. Where Claude followed Joel's stated way of working, it did so cleanly: decisions put to him as tappable choices rather than prose, phone alerts through the agreed channel, the login-and-publish step left to him by design, the repository made public and the README written in his voice on his say-so, and the detail bar taken from him directly — he flew the drone, so his eye is the acceptance test, and the plan says so ("Joel was the pilot… no reference-frame ceremony needed"; sandbox CLAUDE.md).

The corrections number about five, in two kinds. Priority corrections: pausing his running job without asking, which he called "an unacceptable adherence to the goal I set" and which became a standing rule to never re-order his work without asking and to check for co-existence first; and rejecting the reduced-resolution fallback, holding full resolution as non-negotiable. Discipline corrections: "verify the tag" after the unverified image name cost money; the slow-rotation preference he had to state; and the standing, repeatedly reinforced insistence on plain human language on every surface. Each of the five is now in the durable record — the sign of a correction absorbed, not merely acknowledged.

The through-line: Claude's engineering judgment rarely needed correcting, but its deference did — twice it advanced Joel's own goals in a way that overstepped how he wanted them advanced (his running job, his resolution bar). Being right about the destination does not grant authority over the route.

  • Strongest moment: honoring the "Joel is the pilot, his eye is the gate" arrangement — no manufactured acceptance ceremony, the artist's judgment taken as the standard.
  • Weakest moment: overriding a job he had explicitly set, off an exploratory question — the exact overstep the standing rule now forbids.

GRADE: B

TEACHER'S COMMENT: Listens well, learns permanently, and twice forgot that the artist outranks the assistant on the artist's own project. Both lessons are now in writing; the grade rises the term neither recurs.

9Velocity

Throughput across the arc was high, and the standout is July 16: one overnight run produced nine dense tiles, a fused 52-million-point master, ten compact web scenes, and ten published live scenes in a single cycle (deliver\ assets; LIVE_URLS.md). The days that followed kept pace — July 17 added the engine-plugin decision and launched the 36-tile rebuild; July 18 landed the merged solve and pushed the rebuild past two-thirds; July 19 drained the full queue, ran the pilot and its control, and opened both the cloud and game-engine tracks.

Parallelism was, on the whole, used well — and notably better after correction. The merged solve ran on the processor alongside the trainer on the card once Joel pointed out they never competed; the plugin bake-off was processor-only work that cost the queue nothing; and right now the cloud pilot builds its trainer and transfers its dataset at once while a separate track crops tiles for the game engine on the studio processor (cloud-pilot-notes.md, ue-import-notes.md). Two machines, both their processor and graphics lanes worked concurrently — the intended pattern.

The blemishes are the idle pockets, all small relative to throughput: the ten-minute hand-off gap, and the roughly eight-hour window on July 19 when the studio card sat idle after the pilots — bounded because full-resolution work had moved to the cloud and a processor-only crop later picked the box back up, but a real gap while a paid card did the training the idle card could not.

  • Strongest moment: the July 16 overnight cycle — plan to nine tiles to a fused master to ten live scenes in one pass.
  • Weakest moment: the ~8-hour idle studio card on July 19 while the rented card carried the load — bounded and partly unavoidable, but idle high-value hardware all the same.

GRADE: A-

TEACHER'S COMMENT: Nine tiles to ten live scenes in a single night sets the bar for the class. Keep both machines breathing and close the idle pockets faster — a paid card working while an owned card sleeps is a habit to break young.

PRINCIPAL'S NOTE: This is a gifted student with one identified weakness, and the transcript shows both plainly. The gift: in five days, a 2022 drone archive that had defeated a "fishbowl" merge attempt in 2023 became ten published scenes, then thirty-six full-resolution tiles, then a single two-flight solve that loses one frame in 6,589 — with a paper trail so complete a stranger could take over tomorrow. The weakness: eagerness that outruns authority — a paused render, an unwatched meter — costing real money and, more expensively, trust. The correctives are not promises but machinery, and the machinery has already caught failures before they could bill. Promotion to the next term is granted on three conditions: the billing guards stay armed on every meter without exception; the artist's authority over his own priorities is absolute, no matter how good the idea; and the one open exam — the full-resolution combined tile now sitting on a rented card — is completed and judged by the only eye that counts. If the stones read, this transcript will have been the cheap part of something remarkable.
